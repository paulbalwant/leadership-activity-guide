Power & Leadership
Playwriting Assignment

A guide to help your group write, perform, and debrief your short dramatic play

1

Required Structure — Three Parts

⚠ All three parts are mandatory. Omitting the Prologue or the Debrief will result in an incomplete performance. Each part serves a distinct academic purpose.
Part A · Before the play
Prologue
  • Read aloud to the class — not acted
  • Describe the setting — this can be an organisation, a fictional world, a sports team, a historical court, a spaceship crew, a fantasy kingdom, or any context your group chooses
  • Introduce each character's role within that setting
  • Briefly establish the situation or conflict
  • Name the leadership theory your group is portraying
Part B · The performance
The Play (~5 min)
  • Perform a short dramatic scene
  • Can be fictional, based on a real event, or drawn from the textbook
  • Cue cards are acceptable
  • Focus on behaviours — show, don't just tell
  • You may incorporate more than one theory
Part C · After the play
Debrief (~2 min)
  • A brief group lecture to the class
  • Name the specific behaviours you portrayed and link them to theory
  • Invite brief questions or reactions
2

Your Central Focus: Behaviours

Leadership theories are only meaningful when they translate into observable actions. Your play must dramatize what a leader actually does — how they speak, decide, delegate, listen, challenge, and model. Abstract concepts like "vision" or "humility" only land when the audience sees them in action.

Tip: If someone watching your play cannot name at least two specific behaviours without reading your Prologue, your scene is too abstract. Ground every idea in a concrete moment.

Pre-Performance Checklist

Use this before you perform. Items marked ★ are non-negotiable.

  • We have written and will read aloud a Prologue that names the setting, characters, and leadership theory
  • Our play shows at least two specific, named behaviours from our theory
  • We have prepared a 2-minute Debrief that names those behaviours explicitly and links them to theory
  • Our scene runs approximately 5 minutes (time yourselves in rehearsal)
  • Every group member has a speaking role in at least one part of the activity
  • We are not merely describing our theory — we are showing it through action

Group 1 · Behavioural Guide
Group 1

Initiating Structure &
Consideration

Two complementary behavioural styles — one focused on getting the work done, the other on the people doing it. Both are necessary, and both are dramatizable.

Task-Oriented Behaviours to Show

  • Assigning work clearly and specifying each person's responsibilities
  • Setting goals and deadlines, then following up with specific performance feedback
  • Establishing procedures and planning future work
  • Holding team members accountable to clear standards

People-Oriented Behaviours to Show

  • Showing genuine concern for an employee's personal needs or wellbeing
  • Recognising and acknowledging individual contributions publicly
  • Listening actively to concerns without immediately jumping to solutions
  • Taking visible steps to make the work environment more positive or supportive

Scene Suggestion

Contrast is your friend. Show the same leader handling two different situations — one requiring structure (e.g., a missed deadline) and one requiring consideration (e.g., a team member struggling personally). Placing both in the same scene reveals that the two styles are complementary, not interchangeable.

Debrief Pointers

  • Name at least one task-oriented and one people-oriented behaviour you showed
  • Explain why neither style alone is sufficient — this is the core insight of this framework
Group 2 · Behavioural Guide
Group 2

Shared or Distributed
Leadership

Leadership is a role, not a fixed position assigned to one person. Team members lead each other based on expertise, situation, and need.

Key Behaviours to Show

  • A formal leader explicitly delegating authority to a team member for a specific task
  • A peer stepping up to champion an idea or guide others — without being asked
  • Team members influencing each other's decisions through expertise, not position
  • A culture of collaboration (not competition) visible in how characters speak and respond
  • Someone developing their influence skills, actively encouraged by the formal leader

Conditions That Enable Shared Leadership

Your scene can also dramatize why shared leadership works by depicting these enabling conditions:

  • Formal leaders willing to share power — not hoard it
  • A collaborative rather than competitive team culture
  • Employees with developed influence and communication skills

Scene Suggestion

Avoid the committee meeting cliché. Try a self-managing team navigating a sudden crisis — a system failure, a client emergency, a last-minute collapse. Who steps up? How does the formal leader respond when a peer takes charge? The most interesting moment is when a follower leads and the formal leader follows gracefully.

Debrief Pointers

  • Identify the moment in your scene when leadership "shifted" from the formal leader to a peer
  • Explain what made that shift possible — which enabling conditions were present?

Group 3

Transformational /
Visionary Leadership

A vision-focused leader who uses specific behaviours to communicate a compelling future, model it personally, and inspire followers to achieve goals aligned with it.

The Four Elements — Behaviours to Show

  • Develop & communicate the vision: Frame a future state with passion and sincerity; use symbols, metaphors, or stories to make it vivid
  • Model the vision: The leader acts in line with the vision — followers see them "walking the talk," not just preaching it
  • Encourage experimentation: Challenge followers' assumptions; reward those who try new approaches, even if they fail
  • Build commitment: Celebrate progress; give individual recognition; show followers how their work connects to the larger vision

Bass Framework Behaviours

  • Inspirational motivation: Communicating with enthusiasm and optimism; using symbols to focus collective effort
  • Individualised consideration: Treating each follower as a unique individual; giving personalised attention to their specific needs and development
  • Intellectual stimulation: Challenging followers' assumptions and helping them analyse problems from new angles

Scene Suggestion

A leader rallying a demoralised team after a setback. Show how communicating a clear vision, acknowledging individual struggles, and recognising small wins rebuilds commitment. The most powerful scenes show the leader changing someone's emotional state through behaviour — not just a rousing speech.

Debrief Pointers

  • Name which of the four elements (or Bass behaviours) your scene depicted, and how
  • Distinguish transformational leadership from simply being enthusiastic or charismatic — it is defined by specific behaviours, not personality

Group 4

Servant Leadership

The leader's primary purpose is to serve followers — their needs, growth, and development come before the leader's own interests. Think: selfless, humble, empathetic, ethical.

I-SHAPE: Six Dimensions to Dramatize

  • Interpersonal acceptance (I): Responding with warmth, compassion, and forgiveness when a follower makes a mistake — understanding their perspective rather than punishing the error
  • Stewardship (S): Visibly prioritising the team's interests over personal gain, credit, or recognition
  • Humility (H): Openly acknowledging that others know more; giving credit to the team; being genuinely modest about one's own accomplishments
  • Authenticity (A): Expressing honest thoughts and feelings rather than performing authority or maintaining a facade
  • Providing direction (P): Clearly informing followers what is expected — servant leaders still direct, they just do it in service of others' success
  • Empowering & developing (E): Actively coaching, sharing information, and building followers' capabilities rather than creating dependence on the leader

Scene Suggestion

Show service at personal cost. A leader who passes all credit to the team after a major success, then stays behind to mentor a struggling team member on their own time. The key is that the leader's behaviour costs them something — comfort, recognition, time — and they choose the follower's needs anyway.

Debrief Pointers

  • Name at least two I-SHAPE dimensions your scene depicted and how you showed them
  • Address the misconception that servant leaders are "weak" — note that direction and stewardship are also core parts of servant leadership

Group 5

Destructive Leadership

Volitional leader behaviour that genuinely harms followers and/or the organisation — through harmful methods of influence or by pursuing goals that contradict legitimate interests.

Critical Distinction

Destructive leadership is not simply being strict, demanding, or unpopular. It involves genuine harm — psychological, ethical, or organisational. A leader who holds high standards is not automatically destructive. Play this for real, not for laughs.

Behaviours to Show

  • Intimidating, humiliating, or belittling followers — in public or in private
  • Taking credit for others' work; deflecting blame downward onto followers
  • Encouraging followers to act against the organisation's legitimate interests (e.g., covering up problems, cutting ethical corners)
  • Using fear or manipulation as the primary tool of influence rather than legitimate means
  • Silencing dissent — punishing honest feedback or creating an atmosphere where no one dares speak up

Scene Suggestion

Show the consequences, not just the behaviour. The most powerful scenes depict how followers are affected — their silence, fear, disengagement, or ethical compromise. Show the ripple effect on the team or organisation, not only the leader's actions. This is what makes it genuinely "destructive."

Debrief Pointers

  • Distinguish your portrayal from mere strictness — explain why the behaviours cross into genuine harm
  • Note that destructive leadership can coexist with organisational success — some destructive leaders are "effective" by narrow metrics
Group 6 · Behavioural Guide
Group 6

Leading Change

Leading change concerns the process of guiding people through transformation. It draws on multiple leadership styles but has its own distinct behavioural demands at each stage.

Key Behaviours Across the Change Process

  • Creating urgency: Making a compelling case for why change must happen now — what happens if the organisation does not act?
  • Building a coalition: Actively recruiting specific people to champion the change; not waiting for enthusiasm to emerge on its own
  • Communicating the vision: Repeating the vision clearly and consistently — leaders dramatically underestimate how much communication is needed
  • Empowering others to act: Removing obstacles rather than controlling every step; trusting others to execute
  • Generating short-term wins: Identifying and celebrating early successes to build momentum and credibility
  • Acknowledging fears and uncertainty: Recognising what followers are losing, not just what they are gaining — interpersonal acceptance is critical here

Scene Suggestion

Focus on human resistance to change. Show a leader navigating a follower who is resistant, fearful, or openly opposed. How does the leader address those fears without dismissing them? The communication, coalition-building, and urgency stages are especially dramatic and easy to portray in a short scene.

How This Differs from Transformational Leadership

Transformational leadership concerns who the leader is and how they motivate. Leading change concerns the process and stages of guiding people through transformation. Your debrief should focus on the process — not just the leader's vision or personality.

Debrief Pointers

  • Name which stage(s) of the change process your scene depicted
  • Identify the specific leader behaviours shown at each stage
  • Explain how this is distinct from transformational leadership
